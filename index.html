Chapter 1 · Where

Where did the fires burn?

Of the 199,417 fire detections recorded across Australia, New South Wales alone accounted for more than half — a dominance felt from the Blue Mountains to the far south coast. Yet no state was untouched: scan the parliament chart to see each state's share, then follow the streamgraph to watch how the crisis spread and peaked month by month.

Fig. 02One Nation, One Crisis — each flame is 1% of all detections, coloured by state

51 of the 100 flames belong to NSW — a state filling more than half the nation's fire parliament. The remaining 49 are spread across five other states, yet the disparity goes beyond geography: NSW fires were concentrated in densely forested coastal ranges, making each detection more destructive than the diffuse savanna burns that account for WA's and NT's shares.

Fig. 01A Perfect Storm — the six interlocking conditions that made Black Summer possible

Three years of drought, record-breaking heat, and an El Niño-like climate signal primed the landscape; when lightning struck tinder-dry forests under extreme fire weather, the result was inevitable.

Australia recorded its driest year in 2019, with soil moisture at historic lows. Vegetation that normally recovers between fire seasons never did — by spring, forests across the east were loaded with dead fuel ready to ignite.

December 2019 set a new national temperature record of 41.9 °C. Simultaneous extreme fire danger ratings across multiple states were unprecedented, stretching suppression resources beyond capacity.

Climate scientists attributed the heightened fire-weather risk directly to long-term warming. Black Summer was not a single disaster but a signal: the conditions it took to produce this season will become increasingly ordinary.

Prolonged drought Record heat Dry fuels Extreme fire weather Dry lightning Climate change signal

Six interlocking conditions converged to produce the most destructive fire season in Australia's recorded history. No single driver explains Black Summer — it was their simultaneous presence that overwhelmed every natural and human defence. By season's end, roughly 18.6 million hectares had burned and nearly three billion animals were killed or displaced.

Chapter 2 · When

How did the fires escalate over time?

Fire detections crept in at 16,254 in August 2019 — mostly distant grassland burns in Western Australia and the Northern Territory. Then came the east-coast drought, and the numbers kept climbing: 41,308 in October, 47,138 in November, and a catastrophic 81,412 in December — five times the August total and far above anything recorded in the same months between 2005 and 2018. Use the daily chart to trace how fast the crisis built, then select a month on the map below to see exactly where the fires were burning.

Fig. 04Daily Fire Detections — Aug 2019 to Jan 2020, against the 2005–2018 average

Daily detections track the historical rhythm through October, then diverge sharply in November and surge through December and early January. The peak lands on 4 Jan 2020 with 7,342 detections — a single day exceeding anything in the 2005–2018 record.

The season opened deceptively: August and September sat near the ten-year norm. By late October the gap had become unmistakable, and Black Summer surpassed the historical daily ceiling 23 times in December alone.

Chapter 3 · How Intense

How powerful were these fires?

Fire Radiative Power (FRP) measures how much energy a fire pumps into the atmosphere every second — think of it as the fire's wattage. The average detection during Black Summer was 89 MW, roughly the output of a small power station. Yet the season's peak hit 11,164 MW: the equivalent of ten large coal power stations roaring at full capacity at once, all from a single fire cluster.

Fig. 06Fire energy distribution — most fires burn quietly, but a handful are catastrophic

Most detections cluster at low FRP values — the curve peaks sharply near zero and falls away fast. But the long right tail tells a different story: a small fraction of fires exceed 500 MW, releasing so much energy they dominate total heat output despite being rare.

The mean of 89 MW sits well to the right of the distribution peak, pulled upward by extreme outliers — a reminder that average intensity understates the worst of Black Summer.

Chapter 4 · Legacy

Nothing in the record books comes close

Fifteen years of fire data make one thing clear: 2019-20 was not just a bad season — it was in a category of its own. The dot plot and raincloud chart show how far October through December 2019 exceeded anything recorded since 2005. Zoom out to the map and calendar, and the geography and timing of that destruction snaps into focus: NSW bore the heaviest concentration of fire, and December was the single most intense month the country has seen on record.

Historical wildfire records put the season into context. Black Summer stands out sharply across south-eastern Australia, while the raincloud chart shows how unusual late-2019 On the right, Western Australia's fire hotspots are mapped by month — northern savanna burns dominated in August–September, while southern fires intensified through December–January.

Fig. 11Estimated Fire Area by Season — 2019-20 tops every prior year

Each dot is one Aug-to-Jan season across NSW, VIC, QLD, SA and TAS. The red dot for 2019-20 sits above the historical field, edging past the severe 2012-13 season rather than blending into the old range.

The 2012–13 season was the previous record holder, visible as a labelled outlier above the historical cluster. Black Summer exceeded it by a wide margin — a gap that is apparent without any annotation. Every other year in the record sits comfortably below the 2012–13 mark; 2019–20 clears it entirely, reflecting a scale of destruction that overwhelmed standard firefighting capacity.

No upward trend is visible in the historical seasons — fires varied from year to year with no clear direction. Black Summer was not the product of a gradual escalation; it arrived as a sharp discontinuity.
